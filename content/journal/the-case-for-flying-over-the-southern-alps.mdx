The first time I landed on the Tasman Glacier névé, I was 22 and working a summer season in the Mackenzie Basin. The helicopter set down on a flat of ancient compacted ice at about 2,000 metres. The engine stopped. The rotors slowed. And in the silence that followed, the only sounds were the wind off the main divide and, somewhere below, the shifting of ice.

I've been in that silence many times since, with guests who've arrived at that landing carrying 40 years of expectations about what a helicopter flight over mountains looks like. And the response is always the same kind of quiet.

## What this is, and isn't

I want to be direct about the category of experience we're discussing here, because there's a lot of variation under the heading "helicopter flight over New Zealand."

There are 15-minute scenic flights that circle a landmark and return. They're fine. That's not what I'm describing.

I'm describing a 60-90 minute flight that departs from the Mackenzie Basin, tracks up the Tasman Glacier — at 27 kilometres, the longest in the Southern Hemisphere outside the polar regions — lands on the upper névé for time on the ice, and returns via the aiguilles and the Hooker Valley below Aoraki/Mt Cook.

At different points in that flight you're at eye level with peaks that exceed 3,000 metres. You cross the main divide and see the West Coast laid out below you — rainforest and ocean from horizon to horizon. The scale of the Southern Alps from the air is genuinely difficult to process. The topographic model in your head, built from maps and photographs, turns out to be inadequate.

## The Aoraki context

Aoraki/Mt Cook is 3,724 metres — New Zealand's highest mountain, and the one Sir Edmund Hillary used to train for Everest. It's been protected within Aoraki/Mt Cook National Park since 1953. The Māori name, Aoraki, means "cloud piercer" — and watching it emerge from the cloud layer on a clear morning from a helicopter is one of the more visually arresting things available in this country.

The combination of the Māori and English names matters here. Aoraki is the name Te Runanga o Ngāi Tahu (the South Island's principal iwi) holds for this mountain as an ancestor. The landscape you're flying over carries that weight. We mention it not to create solemnity but because understanding it makes the experience fuller.

## How we build it into a journey

The Mackenzie Basin — Tekapo, Twizel, Mt Cook Village — deserves more time than most itineraries give it. We typically suggest two nights: one for arrival and the stargazing (the Mackenzie is one of the world's premier dark sky reserves, and the Milky Way from the Tekapo Observatory at midnight has stopped many people I know), and one for the helicopter flight.

If the itinerary connects south to Fiordland or west to the Franz Josef / Fox Glacier area, the helicopter flight can serve as a transition — landing on the West Coast and being met by ground transport. It's a different kind of arrival.

## What to look for in the operator

The quality of helicopter experiences in New Zealand varies significantly. The distinction is usually in the pilot — specifically, whether they're guiding as well as flying. The pilots we work with are the ones who've flown this terrain for 10 or 20 years, who know what you're looking at before you do, and who will pause the flight when something worth seeing is below you.
