The Great Walks — New Zealand's nine most celebrated long-distance trails — require advance booking that can run six months ahead of season. For guests planning a journey on shorter notice, or for those who simply prefer walking without a crowd, the question becomes: what else is there?

The answer is: quite a lot. New Zealand has an extensive network of well-maintained tracks that don't carry the Great Walk designation but are, in most respects, just as good — and in some cases, better suited to a guest who wants the experience to feel genuinely their own.

These are eight we return to regularly.

## North Island

### Holdsworth-Jumbo Circuit, Tararua Forest Park

The Tararua Ranges have a fearsome reputation for weather — sudden, violent, and uncompromising. But there are well-graded routes that are accessible to confident walkers, and the Holdsworth-Jumbo Circuit is the best of them.

The route climbs steeply through native bush to Powell Hut — a modern facility that feels significantly better appointed than the old structure it replaced, which was held down by wire cables against the wind. From there, the track opens onto tussock slopes with coast-to-coast views, continuing to Jumbo Hut before dropping back through one of the finest examples of goblin forest in the North Island — the twisted, moss-draped beech that grows at altitude — and down to a river valley before returning to the Holdsworth Campsite.

Allow two days. The effort is real, but the Tararuas on a clear day are genuinely extraordinary.

### Tarawera Trail, Rotorua

Lake Tarawera is one of the darker, more atmospheric lakes in the North Island — deep blue-black, rimmed by bush and the scarred slopes of the mountain that erupted here in 1886, devastating the valley and burying the famous Pink and White Terraces. The trail follows the lake shore through regenerated bush over old lava flows, crossing streams fed by natural springs along a route that Te Arawa have walked for centuries.

The reward at the end of the trail is a beach campsite with natural hot pools, which should be taken seriously as a destination in itself. Bring a spade to dig out your own pool at the water's edge, a tent, and plan to return the same way the next morning.

Five hours from the car park. Best done as an overnight.

### Pouakai Circuit, Taranaki

The Pouakai Circuit gives you the closest possible view of Mt Taranaki without requiring a summit attempt. The route begins with a steep climb — locals call the first section the Puffer — before contouring along the dramatic volcanic face of the mountain through diverse mosses, deep gorges, and historic lava flows that reveal themselves properly only once you're moving through them.

The middle section crosses the Ahukawakawa Swamp, a kidney-shaped wetland that is both tranquil and slightly unsettling in equal measure. From the upper sections, on a clear day, the views extend north and west for hundreds of kilometres, with New Plymouth visible far below.

Don't get so taken by the panorama that you walk into the swamp. It happens.

### Cape Brett, Bay of Islands

A two-day coastal tramp traversing 16 kilometres along the Rawhiti peninsula to the red-roofed Cape Brett Hut, formerly the lighthouse keeper's quarters at the end of the headland. The setting is extraordinary — teeming with seabirds and wildlife, with a view from the dining table that extends across open ocean.

The track is exposed in places and requires some fitness. Experienced walkers only. Trampers are currently advised to carry their own water, as the tank supply at the hut has salt contamination.

## South Island

### St James Walkway, Canterbury

If the Tararuas are about the effort, the St James Walkway is the opposite proposition: long, mostly flat, and surrounded by one of the most compelling mountain landscapes in the South Island. The 66-kilometre route through the Lewis Pass high country covers tall beech forest, alpine scrub, wide braided rivers, tussock fields, and a sequence of historic musterer's huts.

Four days is achievable with good planning and the long southern daylight. This is an excellent introduction to South Island multi-day tramping for guests who want serious scenery without serious terrain.

### Copland Track, West Coast

The West Coast has a specific quality of light and vegetation — dense, mossy, permanently damp, haunting — that no other region in New Zealand quite replicates. The Copland Track takes you into it properly: a gradual climb following a roaring creek up into a narrowing valley, through forest that feels like it belongs to an earlier version of the world, towards the lower reaches of the Southern Alps.

Welcome Flat Hut at the end of the accessible section of the track is large and comfortable, and the natural hot pools a short walk from the hut are the primary reason most people do this walk. The views back down the valley from the pools, with the mountain range surrounding you and kea quarrelling overhead, make the return journey the next morning feel entirely different from the approach.

Two days return.

### Travers-Sabine Circuit, Nelson Lakes National Park

A five-day loop for fit and experienced walkers, but one of the most varied in the country. You begin on Lake Rotoiti — either walking the shoreline or taking the water taxi — before following the Travers Valley through beech forest of extraordinary greenness, climbing to the alpine zone over Travers Saddle with its kea, then descending into the Sabine Valley to Lake Rotoroa before the final climb out to the Mt Robert car park.

The circuit has something for every kind of tramping: water, forest, alpine, and the valley scale that the Nelson Lakes landscape does better than anywhere.

### Rees-Dart Track, Mt Aspiring National Park

The Rees-Dart is the South Island's answer to the Routeburn — comparable scenery, a fraction of the traffic. Located 68 kilometres from Queenstown via Glenorchy, the 63-kilometre circuit takes four to five days and covers sweeping valleys, beech and alpine vegetation, and glacier-fed rivers through the Mt Aspiring wilderness.

This is an advanced track for experienced walkers. Build in a day at Dart Hut for the excursion to Dart Glacier and Cascade Saddle — the views back down the Matukituki River from the saddle are among the finest in the Southern Alps.

Also kea-averse guests should consider themselves warned: the Dart Hut population is significant and uninhibited.

## How we incorporate these

Walking is part of almost every journey we design — the question is how much, and what kind. The tracks above are not the famous names, but they're the ones where you're less likely to meet a crowd at the hut, where the experience feels more proportional to the effort, and where the landscape tends to stay with guests after they've left.

If walking is central to your trip, tell us early. Some of these tracks need specific planning around hut availability, transport, and logistics that we can sort in advance.
