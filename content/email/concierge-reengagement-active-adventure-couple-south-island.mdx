Kia ora,

It has been a little while since we spoke — you had mentioned a 12-day South Island journey, built around the kind of active, immersive experiences that make this part of the world feel less like a destination and more like a conversation you step into willingly, all senses open.

A glacier helicopter landing. Guided hiking through landscapes that shift from rainforest to alpine inside a single morning. An overnight kayak on Milford Sound, where the water goes still after the day boats leave and the fiord becomes something quieter, deeper, older — the kind of place that does not perform, but simply exists, and asks you to slow down enough to meet it on its own terms. And the Marlborough Sounds, where the Queen Charlotte Track winds through native bush above sheltered waterways you could spend a lifetime exploring and never quite feel you had finished.

That is not a holiday checklist. That is a journey with weight. And it happens to describe, with uncanny precision, the things we care about most when we curate time in the South Island.

We have been doing this for a long time through our parent company, PPG Tours. The relationships we have built with the country's finest helicopter pilots, wilderness guides, and the small lodges that sit in landscapes you do not forget — these are the foundation of everything we arrange. Nothing is off-the-shelf. Every day is shaped around you.

The glacier helicopter landing you mentioned — I am thinking of Franz Josef or Fox Glacier, on the West Coast, where you lift off from the rainforest floor and within minutes are standing on blue ice, the Southern Alps stretching in every direction. Some of our guests combine the helicopter with a guided walk on the glacier itself, crampons on, stepping across crevasses. Others prefer to land on the nevé, walk on fresh snow, and fly back out over peaks you will spend the rest of the journey trying to describe. Both are extraordinary. Both depend on what the weather gives you and what feels right on the day.

The guided hiking — there are routes that range from a half-day through ancient beech forest to multi-day sections of the Great Walks. The Routeburn Track, crossing from valley to alpine saddle in a day. The Kepler, with its ridgeline above Lake Te Anau. The Hooker Valley beneath Aoraki / Mount Cook, a gentle walk that ends at a glacial lake with icebergs floating in it, even in high summer. Each has its own character. Each rewards the people who give it time rather than rush through.

The overnight kayak on Milford Sound is one of the most honest ways to experience Fiordland. You paddle into reaches the cruise boats cannot access. You sleep on the water. You wake to mist lying low on the fiord and the sound of waterfalls falling without competition for your attention. It is not for everyone — it asks something of you physically — but for the people who choose it, it is the part of the journey they carry longest. The part they talk about years later.

And the Marlborough Sounds — the Queen Charlotte Track, the sheltered bays, the green-lipped mussels steamed open on a boat with a glass of local sauvignon blanc in hand, the water so clear you can see the seabed from the deck. That corner of the South Island has a warmth to it, a generosity, that balances the drama of the west coast and the alps. It is the exhale after the in-breath.
